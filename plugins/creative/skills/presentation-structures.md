## 1. Presentation Type Frameworks

### Pitch Presentation (Investor/Sales)

**Purpose**: Persuade stakeholders to take action (invest, buy, partner)

**Core Structure** (10-15 slides):
1. Hook - Compelling problem statement or vision
2. Problem - Pain point with evidence
3. Solution - Your unique approach
4. Value Proposition - Why you, why now
5. Market Opportunity - Size and timing
6. Business Model - How money flows
7. Traction/Proof - Evidence it works
8. Competitive Landscape - Your differentiation
9. Team - Why you can execute
10. Ask - Specific request with terms
11. Appendix - Supporting details

**Example Opening**:
```
Slide 1: "Every day, 10,000 small businesses close because
they cannot access the capital they need to survive."
[Single statistic, full screen, dramatic pause]
```

**Key Principles**:
- Lead with emotion, support with data
- One idea per slide maximum
- Every slide must answer "So what?"
- Build to a clear, specific ask

---

### Status Update Presentation

**Purpose**: Inform stakeholders on progress, blockers, and next steps

**Core Structure** (5-10 slides):
1. Executive Summary - TL;DR in 3 bullets
2. Progress vs. Plan - Visual timeline/roadmap
3. Key Wins - Celebrate achievements
4. Blockers/Risks - What needs attention
5. Metrics Dashboard - KPIs at a glance
6. Next Period Goals - Clear deliverables
7. Support Needed - Specific asks
8. Q&A Prompt

**Example Status Slide**:
```
PROJECT ALPHA - WEEK 12

On Track: [GREEN]  3 items
At Risk:  [YELLOW] 1 item
Blocked:  [RED]    0 items

Key Win: Shipped v2.1 ahead of schedule
Watch:   API partner delayed 2 weeks
```

**Key Principles**:
- Lead with the bottom line (BLUF)
- Use consistent RAG status indicators
- Focus on exceptions, not routine
- Quantify everything possible

---

### Proposal Presentation

**Purpose**: Recommend a course of action for decision-makers

**Core Structure** (12-15 slides):
1. Context - Why are we here?
2. Objective - What decision is needed?
3. Current State - Where we are today
4. Problem/Opportunity - Gap analysis
5. Options Considered - 2-4 alternatives
6. Recommended Option - Your proposal
7. Supporting Evidence - Data and rationale
8. Implementation Plan - Timeline and resources
9. Risk Assessment - What could go wrong
10. Mitigation Strategies - How we will manage risks
11. Investment Required - Cost breakdown
12. Expected ROI - Benefits and timeline
13. Decision Request - Specific approval needed

**Example Recommendation Slide**:
```
RECOMMENDATION: Option B - Phased Rollout

Why Option B:
- 40% lower risk than full launch
- Allows learning before scale
- Fits within Q3 budget

Trade-off: 6-week longer timeline
Mitigated by: Parallel workstreams in Phase 2
```

---

### Educational/Training Presentation

**Purpose**: Transfer knowledge and build skills

**Core Structure**:
1. Learning Objectives - What they will gain
2. Relevance Hook - Why this matters to them
3. Agenda/Roadmap - Journey overview
4. Concept Introduction - New information
5. Example/Demonstration - Show it in action
6. Practice Activity - Hands-on application
7. Check for Understanding - Quick assessment
8. Summary - Key takeaways
9. Resources - Where to learn more
10. Q&A

**Key Principles**:
- Chunk content into 10-15 minute segments
- Include interaction every 7-10 slides
- Use concrete examples before abstract principles
- Repeat key concepts 3 times minimum

---

### Strategic Planning Presentation

**Purpose**: Align organization on direction and priorities

**Core Structure**:
1. Vision Reminder - Where we are going
2. Environmental Scan - External factors
3. Internal Assessment - Capabilities/gaps
4. Strategic Priorities - 3-5 focus areas
5. Priority Deep Dives - One section each
6. Resource Allocation - How we will invest
7. Success Metrics - How we will measure
8. Timeline/Milestones - When we will deliver
9. Governance - How we will manage
10. Commitment Ask - Alignment request

## 2. Story Arc Structures

### The Classic Three-Act Structure

```
ACT I: SETUP (25%)
├── Hook: Grab attention immediately
├── Context: Establish the world/situation
├── Stakes: Why this matters
└── Transition: The inciting incident

ACT II: CONFRONTATION (50%)
├── Challenges: Obstacles and conflicts
├── Rising Action: Building tension
├── Data/Evidence: Supporting your narrative
└── Turning Point: The key insight

ACT III: RESOLUTION (25%)
├── Solution: Your recommendation
├── Transformation: Before vs. after
├── Call to Action: What you need from them
└── Memorable Close: Final impression
```

---

### The Hero's Journey (Simplified for Business)

1. **Ordinary World** - Current state, business as usual
2. **Call to Adventure** - The opportunity or threat
3. **Refusal of Call** - Why change is hard
4. **Meeting the Mentor** - The insight or framework
5. **Crossing the Threshold** - Committing to action
6. **Tests and Allies** - Challenges and supporters
7. **The Ordeal** - The critical challenge
8. **The Reward** - What success looks like
9. **The Return** - Bringing value back
10. **The Transformation** - New normal achieved

**Example Application**:
```
"Our customers [Hero] were drowning in spreadsheets [Ordinary World].
They knew there had to be a better way [Call].
But switching tools felt risky [Refusal].
Then they discovered our methodology [Mentor].
After one pilot team tried it [Threshold]...
Despite initial pushback [Tests]...
And a major data migration challenge [Ordeal]...
They reduced reporting time by 80% [Reward].
Now the entire company uses it [Return].
They went from reactive to strategic [Transformation]."
```

---

### Problem-Solution-Benefit (PSB)

```
PROBLEM (30%)
├── What is broken/missing/painful?
├── Who is affected and how?
├── What is the cost of inaction?
└── Evidence: Data, stories, examples

SOLUTION (40%)
├── What is your proposed approach?
├── How does it work?
├── Why will it succeed?
└── Proof: Case studies, pilots, research

BENEFIT (30%)
├── What will be different?
├── Quantified outcomes
├── Timeline to value
└── Call to action
```

---

### The Pyramid Principle (Minto)

Start with the answer, then support with grouped arguments.

```
MAIN MESSAGE (Slide 1)
"We should acquire Company X"

SUPPORTING ARGUMENTS (Slides 2-4)
├── Argument 1: Strategic fit
│   ├── Supporting point
│   ├── Supporting point
│   └── Supporting point
├── Argument 2: Financial returns
│   ├── Supporting point
│   ├── Supporting point
│   └── Supporting point
└── Argument 3: Low execution risk
    ├── Supporting point
    ├── Supporting point
    └── Supporting point

CONCLUSION (Final Slide)
Restate main message with call to action
```

---

### The SCQA Framework

```
SITUATION: What is the current context?
"Our market share has grown 15% annually for 5 years."

COMPLICATION: What changed or threatens this?
"A new competitor launched with 40% lower prices."

QUESTION: What is the key question this raises?
"How do we maintain growth in this new environment?"

ANSWER: Your recommended response
"We differentiate on service and expand to adjacent markets."
```

## 3. Slide Types and Purposes

### Title Slide
**Purpose**: Set the stage, establish credibility
**Contains**: Presentation title, subtitle, presenter name, date, company logo
**Design**: Clean, branded, minimal text

### Agenda Slide
**Purpose**: Set expectations, provide roadmap
**Contains**: 3-7 section titles with time estimates
**Design**: Clear hierarchy, possibly visual timeline

### Section Divider
**Purpose**: Signal transitions, allow mental reset
**Contains**: Section title, brief context
**Design**: Bold, simple, consistent styling

### Content Slide (Text-Heavy)
**Purpose**: Communicate detailed information
**Contains**: Headline, 3-5 bullet points, optional visual
**Design**: Clear hierarchy, limit to 6 lines of text

### Data Slide
**Purpose**: Present evidence and analysis
**Contains**: Chart/graph, insight headline, source citation
**Design**: One visualization per slide, clear labels

### Comparison Slide
**Purpose**: Evaluate options or show contrast
**Contains**: Side-by-side or matrix layout
**Design**: Consistent columns, clear differentiators

### Quote Slide
**Purpose**: Add credibility, human element
**Contains**: Quote text, attribution, optional photo
**Design**: Large text, plenty of white space

### Image Slide
**Purpose**: Emotional impact, visual break
**Contains**: Full-bleed image, minimal text overlay
**Design**: High-quality imagery, readable text contrast

### Process/Timeline Slide
**Purpose**: Show sequence or progression
**Contains**: Steps, phases, or dates with descriptions
**Design**: Clear flow direction, numbered steps

### Summary/Takeaway Slide
**Purpose**: Reinforce key messages
**Contains**: 3-5 main points, action items
**Design**: Scannable format, numbered or bulleted

### Call-to-Action Slide
**Purpose**: Drive specific next steps
**Contains**: Clear ask, how to respond, timeline
**Design**: Prominent CTA, contact information

### Q&A Slide
**Purpose**: Invite discussion
**Contains**: Question prompt, contact details
**Design**: Simple, welcoming, not cluttered

### Appendix Slides
**Purpose**: Support deep-dive questions
**Contains**: Detailed data, methodology, backup
**Design**: Labeled clearly as appendix, numbered

## 4. Content Density Guidelines

### The 1-6-6 Rule
- 1 main idea per slide
- 6 lines of text maximum
- 6 words per line maximum

### The 10-20-30 Rule (Guy Kawasaki)
- 10 slides maximum for a pitch
- 20 minutes maximum duration
- 30pt minimum font size

### Content Density by Slide Type

| Slide Type | Words | Elements | Time |
|------------|-------|----------|------|
| Title | 10-15 | 2-3 | 30 sec |
| Agenda | 20-40 | 4-7 | 1 min |
| Content | 40-60 | 3-5 | 2 min |
| Data | 20-30 | 1-2 | 2-3 min |
| Quote | 15-30 | 1 | 1 min |
| Image | 0-10 | 1 | 30 sec |
| Summary | 30-50 | 3-5 | 1-2 min |

### Density by Audience Type

**Executive Audience**:
- 25% fewer words than standard
- Headlines only, no paragraphs
- Data summarized to single metrics
- Expect interruptions and questions

**Technical Audience**:
- Can handle 50% more density
- Include methodology details
- Show data sources and caveats
- Provide appendix for deep dives

**General Audience**:
- Standard density guidelines
- Define all jargon
- Use analogies and examples
- More visuals, less text

## 5. Visual Hierarchy on Slides

### The Z-Pattern
Eyes naturally scan in a Z pattern:
```
TOP-LEFT (Start here) ───────► TOP-RIGHT
                              │
                              ▼
BOTTOM-LEFT ◄─────────────── BOTTOM-RIGHT (End/CTA)
```

Place most important content at start and end points.

### The F-Pattern (Text-Heavy Slides)
Eyes scan text content in an F pattern:
```
████████████████████
████████████████
█████████████
████████
```

Front-load important information in first words.

### Hierarchy Principles

**Size**: Larger = more important
```
36pt: Slide headline
24pt: Key point/subhead
18pt: Supporting text
14pt: Source/footnote
```

**Position**: Top > Bottom, Left > Right (Western audiences)

**Color**: Saturated > Desaturated, Contrasting > Blending

**Weight**: Bold > Regular > Light

### Example Hierarchy Structure
```
[HEADLINE: What You Need to Remember]     ← 36pt, Bold

[Subhead: The key insight]                ← 24pt, Semi-bold

• Supporting point one with detail        ← 18pt, Regular
• Supporting point two with detail
• Supporting point three with detail

[Source: Company Research, 2024]          ← 12pt, Light, Gray
```

### White Space Rules
- Minimum 10% margins on all sides
- Line spacing at 1.2-1.5x font size
- Paragraph spacing at 1.5-2x line spacing
- Element spacing proportional to relationship

## 6. Data Visualization Selection

### Chart Selection Matrix

| Data Relationship | Best Chart Types |
|-------------------|------------------|
| Comparison (items) | Bar, Column, Dot plot |
| Comparison (time) | Line, Area, Column |
| Composition (static) | Pie, Stacked bar, Treemap |
| Composition (change) | Stacked area, 100% stacked bar |
| Distribution | Histogram, Box plot, Scatter |
| Relationship | Scatter, Bubble, Heatmap |
| Trend | Line, Sparkline |
| Part-to-whole | Pie (max 5), Donut, Waterfall |
| Ranking | Horizontal bar, Lollipop |
| Flow | Sankey, Funnel |

### Chart Best Practices

**Bar Charts**:
- Horizontal for long labels
- Start axis at zero
- Order meaningfully (size, category, time)
- Highlight the key bar

**Line Charts**:
- Use for continuous data only
- Limit to 4-5 lines maximum
- Label lines directly, not in legend
- Show confidence intervals when relevant

**Pie Charts**:
- Maximum 5 slices
- Start largest slice at 12 o'clock
- Use sparingly (bars often better)
- Always include percentages

### Data Slide Structure
```
[INSIGHT HEADLINE: Sales grew 40% in Q3]    ← State the takeaway

[CHART]                                      ← Prove it visually
   • Highlight the key data point
   • Mute supporting context
   • Remove chartjunk

[Source: Sales Database, Oct 2024]           ← Cite your data
```

### Color in Data Visualization
- Gray for context, color for focus
- Sequential palette for continuous data
- Diverging palette for positive/negative
- Categorical palette for distinct groups
- Colorblind-safe palettes always

## 7. Opening and Closing Patterns

### Powerful Opening Patterns

**The Shocking Statistic**
```
"Every 39 seconds, a cyber attack occurs somewhere in the world.
[pause]
Your company is not immune."
```

**The Provocative Question**
```
"What if I told you that 70% of your customers
would leave you for a competitor who remembers their name?"
```

**The Story Hook**
```
"Last Tuesday, a customer called our support line in tears.
She had lost years of family photos.
What happened next changed how we think about backup."
```

**The Bold Claim**
```
"I am going to show you how to double your conversion rate
in the next 90 days with zero additional ad spend."
```

**The Shared Experience**
```
"Raise your hand if you have ever lost an entire day
to meetings that should have been emails.
[pause for response]
Today, I will give you that time back."
```

**The Historical Parallel**
```
"In 1995, Kodak had 145,000 employees.
In 2012, they filed for bankruptcy.
What happened? They invented digital photography
and failed to act on it.
Today's question: What are you ignoring?"
```

---

### Powerful Closing Patterns

**The Callback**
```
Opening: "What would you do with an extra hour every day?"
Closing: "So now you know how to get that hour back.
What will you do with it?"
```

**The Clear Ask**
```
"I need three things from you today:
One, approve the $50K budget.
Two, assign two engineers by Friday.
Three, schedule our kickoff for next Monday.
Can I count on your support?"
```

**The Vision Forward**
```
"Imagine it is December 2025.
We have launched in three new markets.
Customer satisfaction is at an all-time high.
Revenue has doubled.
That future starts with the decision you make today."
```

**The Challenge**
```
"You have heard the data.
You have seen the opportunity.
The only question remaining is:
Are you ready to lead, or will you watch others pass you by?"
```

**The Summary Triad**
```
"Remember three things from today:
First, the market is shifting faster than ever.
Second, we have the team to respond.
Third, the window to act is now.
Thank you."
```

**The Memorable Quote**
```
"As Steve Jobs said, 'The people who are crazy enough
to think they can change the world are the ones who do.'
Let's be crazy together.
Thank you."
```

## 8. Transition Techniques

### Verbal Transitions

**Building Transitions**:
- "Building on that foundation..."
- "Taking this one step further..."
- "This leads us to an important question..."

**Contrasting Transitions**:
- "However, there is another side to consider..."
- "That said, we also need to address..."
- "While this is true, we cannot ignore..."

**Sequential Transitions**:
- "Having established X, let us turn to Y..."
- "The second factor to consider is..."
- "Moving forward in our timeline..."

**Summarizing Transitions**:
- "What this means for us is..."
- "The key takeaway here is..."
- "To put this in perspective..."

### Visual Transitions

**Section Dividers**:
Use bold divider slides to signal major topic shifts
```
[Full-color background]
[Section Title in large white text]
[Brief context in smaller text]
```

**Progressive Reveal**:
Build complex diagrams piece by piece across slides
- Slide 1: Component A
- Slide 2: A + B with connection
- Slide 3: A + B + C with full system

**Visual Callbacks**:
Return to a key visual with additions/changes
- Introduction: Show roadmap with all phases gray
- Each section: Same roadmap with current phase highlighted
- Conclusion: Full roadmap, all phases complete

### Transition Timing
- Pause 1-2 seconds before major transitions
- Use physical movement (step to different area)
- Change vocal tone slightly
- Allow audience to "arrive" at new topic

## 9. Audience-Specific Adaptations

### C-Suite Executives

**Characteristics**:
- Time-constrained (expect interruptions)
- Strategic focus
- Decision-making mindset
- Pattern recognition

**Adaptations**:
- Lead with recommendation
- Bottom-line first (BLUF)
- Quantify everything
- Prepare for out-of-order navigation
- Have executive summary as backup slide 2

**Structure**:
```
Slide 1: Title
Slide 2: Recommendation + Key Numbers
Slide 3: Context (if they want it)
Slides 4-N: Supporting detail (if they want it)
Final: Decision/Ask
```

---

### Technical Audiences

**Characteristics**:
- Detail-oriented
- Skeptical of claims
- Process-focused
- Appreciate precision

**Adaptations**:
- Include methodology
- Show your work
- Define assumptions
- Provide data sources
- Include technical appendix

**Structure**:
```
Slide 1: Title
Slide 2: Problem Statement (precise)
Slides 3-5: Technical Approach
Slides 6-8: Evidence + Methodology
Slide 9: Limitations/Caveats
Slide 10: Conclusions
Appendix: Detailed data, code, specs
```

---

### Sales Teams

**Characteristics**:
- Action-oriented
- Competitive
- Results-focused
- Time-pressed

**Adaptations**:
- Show what is in it for them
- Use competitive framing
- Include talk tracks they can use
- Provide leave-behinds
- Celebrate wins and name names

**Structure**:
```
Slide 1: Hook (quota-relevant)
Slide 2: The New Weapon
Slides 3-5: How It Works
Slide 6: Success Story
Slide 7: Objection Handling
Slide 8: Your Talk Track
Slide 9: Get Started Today
```

---

### Investors

**Characteristics**:
- Pattern matching
- Risk-aware
- Return-focused
- Time-limited

**Adaptations**:
- Address the elephant in the room
- Show you know your numbers
- Demonstrate market timing
- Prove team can execute
- Be specific about use of funds

**Structure**:
```
Standard pitch deck (see Section 1)
Plus: Why Now timing slide
Plus: Use of Funds breakdown
Plus: Deal terms clarity
```

---

### Board of Directors

**Characteristics**:
- Governance-focused
- Long-term thinking
- Risk-conscious
- Fiduciary responsibility

**Adaptations**:
- Frame as strategic decisions
- Show scenario planning
- Address risk mitigation
- Provide competitive context
- Allow for deliberation

**Structure**:
```
Slide 1: Title
Slide 2: Executive Summary
Slide 3: Strategic Context
Slides 4-6: Key Issues/Decisions
Slides 7-9: Options + Recommendation
Slide 10: Risk Assessment
Slide 11: Governance Implications
Slide 12: Decision Request
```

---

### Cross-Functional Teams

**Characteristics**:
- Varied expertise levels
- Different success metrics
- Collaboration-focused
- Need shared context

**Adaptations**:
- Define all jargon
- Show how pieces connect
- Acknowledge different perspectives
- Clarify roles and responsibilities
- Create shared vocabulary

## Quick Reference: Presentation Checklist

### Before Building
- [ ] Who is the audience?
- [ ] What do they care about?
- [ ] What is the one thing they should remember?
- [ ] What action do I want them to take?
- [ ] How much time do I have?

### While Building
- [ ] Does every slide support the main message?
- [ ] Is there one idea per slide?
- [ ] Is the font readable from the back of the room?
- [ ] Does every data slide have an insight headline?
- [ ] Are transitions clear and logical?

### Before Presenting
- [ ] Have I rehearsed out loud?
- [ ] Do I know the first 30 seconds cold?
- [ ] Is backup material ready in appendix?
- [ ] Have I tested technology?
- [ ] Do I have a hard copy backup?

### Timing Guidelines
| Slides | Comfortable Duration |
|--------|---------------------|
| 5-10 | 10-15 minutes |
| 10-15 | 20-30 minutes |
| 15-20 | 30-45 minutes |
| 20-30 | 45-60 minutes |

*Rule of thumb: 2-3 minutes per slide average, less for simple slides, more for complex ones.*
